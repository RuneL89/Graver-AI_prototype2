# Procurement Fraud

## Summary

Manipulation of public procurement processes for personal or corporate gain, including bid-rigging, kickbacks, false invoicing, and conflicts of interest.

## Details

Procurement fraud encompasses a range of illegal schemes designed to corrupt government purchasing. Common methods include:
- **Bid rigging**: Competitors collude to predetermine the winner
- **Kickbacks**: Vendor pays secret commissions to officials or decision-makers
- **False invoicing**: Billing for goods or services never delivered
- **Conflict of interest**: Decision-makers have undisclosed financial stakes in vendors
- **Splitting contracts**: Breaking large contracts into smaller pieces to evade competitive bidding thresholds

Jens Hansen was sanctioned on 2025-11-14 for alleged involvement in procurement fraud (source: sanctions_list_march_2026.pdf). The specific nature of the fraud is not detailed in the sanctions list, but the corporate context provides strong leads:
- Hansen directed two companies that won major government contracts in 2025 (source: business_registry_q1_2026.pdf, procurement_contracts_2025.pdf)
- He signed contract PC-2025-00991 (USD 1,850,000) just weeks before his sanctions (source: procurement_contracts_2025.pdf)
- He used an alias ("Director Y") in one registry entry, suggesting identity obfuscation (source: business_registry_q1_2026.pdf)

These indicators — dual directorship, alias usage, timing of contract signature relative to sanctions — are consistent with patterns seen in procurement fraud cases where insiders manipulate bidding processes to benefit companies they secretly control.
