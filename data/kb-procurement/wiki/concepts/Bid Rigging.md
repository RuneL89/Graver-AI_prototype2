# Bid Rigging

## Summary

An illegal form of collusion where competitors coordinate to manipulate the outcome of a bidding process.

## Details

Bid rigging occurs when two or more competitors agree in advance who will win a contract, often by submitting deliberately high or non-compliant bids to create the appearance of competition (source: procurement_contracts_2025.pdf). It is a form of [[procurement fraud]] that distorts markets, wastes public funds, and undermines trust in government contracting.

Common bid-rigging schemes include:
- **Cover bidding**: Losers submit high bids to guarantee the pre-selected winner
- **Bid suppression**: Some competitors agree not to bid
- **Bid rotation**: Competitors take turns winning contracts
- **Complementary bidding**: Losers submit bids with intentional flaws

In the current dataset, the two largest contracts (PC-2025-00442 and PC-2025-00991) were awarded to companies sharing a director, Jens Hansen (source: business_registry_q1_2026.pdf). While this does not prove bid rigging, it is a significant red flag: if the same individual influences both bidding companies, there may be no genuine competition.
